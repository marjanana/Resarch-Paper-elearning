Authors: K. Duran, JS Flores, A. Marjanovic

Figure: Seated Couple

 

 

 

Among the many collections at New York City’s Metropolitan Museum of Art, every piece of work is a commentary of some subject within the period and culture that produced them. One such subject is the interdependence of man and woman in a relationship communicated through Figure: Seated Couple from West Sudan. Even though the Western societies have a long history of male dominance in the family sphere, the composition of visual elements in Figure: Seated Couple accentuates its culture's outlook on the interdependence of the male and the female. Using abstraction and balance in Figure: Seated Couple, the Dogon people express their views on a male/female partnership.

Figure: Seated Couple is made of wood and iron and represents stylized figures seated side by side on a stool. Created in the 18th and early 19th century, the sculpture was likely used during funeral processions.[1]  According to Walter Van Beek, the sitting posture signifies authority and one's harmony with the world. [2] The use of geometric forms is pronounced. Even though the figures are formally similar, there are iconographic details that set them apart: a small child on the female's back and a quiver on the male's back.

The geometric abstraction of the figures in Seated Couple is used to implement the idea of equal responsibilities of man and woman in Dogon Society. The figure’s torsos and limbs are composed of elongated cylinders, and their chest and hips are made up of round forms. Through their elongated vertical and horizontal forms are given subtle features to differentiate their gender, such as enlarged female breasts. These disproportional geometric forms are, “linked together in this figure to create a tightly structured composition that makes evident the interdependence and symmetry of man's and woman’s roles in Dogon society.”[3] This abstraction of the human body achieved by distributing mass through vertical and horizontal geometric forms (and not through the emphasis of gender or sex), contributes that the viewer perceives the coupled figures as entities that are equal. Such abstraction exemplified the Dogon people’s view of relationships as an equal partnership.

Besides their abstract form, the figures are also arranged in a symmetrically balanced composition. The figures, similar in posture, volume, and proportion, are seated on a bench together. Not only are the figures’ forms balanced out, but also are the iconographic elements: “on the male figure, a beard extends the chin while the female figure wears a labret, an ornament in the lip…On the reverse side, the female figure carries a baby on her back, the male a quiver.”[4] This pronounced symmetry symbolizes and equalizes the gender roles of the figures: the female is represented as a caregiver and the male as a hunter. Neither’s role is more important, as the symmetrical arrangement of the composition suggests a lack of hierarchy.   Thus, the use of balance as a visual and iconographic element demonstrates the outlook of the Dogon people on relationships as partnerships of equal roles.

In conclusion, through its rendering of form and balance, The Seated Couple communicates the Dogon peoples' view of the interdependence of man and woman. The stylized forms translate the rigid posture into structures that support each other, and the symmetrical balance of the composition express the interdependent equality of the figures.
